# The Kindness Flywheel — Target Personas

---

## **Persona 1: The Threatened CTO**

### Demographics & Context
- **Title:** VP Engineering, CTO, Head of Engineering at Series B-D SaaS/fintech/healthtech company
- **Age:** 35-50
- **Experience:** 12-20 years in tech, 3-8 years in current role
- **Team size:** Managing 15-80 engineers
- **Technical depth:** Still codes or reviews code, not pure management

### The Situation
Built the original technical moat that attracted investors and customers. That moat is eroding faster than expected. Commoditization is real—new competitors can build 80% of your product in 1/3 the time.

### What Keeps Them Up at Night
- **The anxiety:** "Are we moving fast enough? Will the next reorg eliminate my role?"
- **The contradiction:** More automation should make things faster, but it's making teams *more* anxious. Attrition is up. The best people are exploring options.
- **The paradox:** Engineering metrics (velocity, PRs merged, deployment frequency) are up. But product-market fit is fuzzy. Customer retention is declining.
- **The identity threat:** Built identity on being the technical leader who "sees five steps ahead." But five steps ahead is unclear now.

### Current Thinking & Solutions
- "We need to move faster. Automate more. Keep the engineers we have by giving them interesting technical problems."
- Probably investing in: better CI/CD, more automation, maybe adding an ML team
- Reading: Engineering blogs, Hacker News, maybe some strategy pieces (McKinsey, BCG)
- Conversations: Peers in the engineering community, maybe a coach (if they're self-aware enough)
- **What they've tried:** Reorg to improve velocity. Invested in better tooling. Hired senior engineers. None of it moved the needle.

### Key Decision Drivers
1. **Data/proof:** McKinsey report, MIT Sloan comment sections. They believe research.
2. **Credibility from peer experience:** If another CTO says "this worked," they listen.
3. **Threat mitigation:** Needs a narrative for the board. "We're investing in X" sounds better than "we're hoping."
4. **Sustainable path:** They're tired. Don't want another sprint, another reorg. Want something that feels like progress, not exhaustion.

### Objections & Resistance
- **"Kindness sounds soft"** → Will dismiss unless you lead with moat/competitive advantage
- **"This is just culture stuff"** → Needs to see the business case (retention costs, async effectiveness, execution velocity)
- **"We already do this"** → Might be skeptical. Needs concrete examples of what "kindness as a system" actually looks like operationally
- **"I don't have time for philosophy"** → Needs practical, implementable immediately
- **"HR already owns culture"** → Needs to understand this is CTO/engineering-specific, not an HR initiative

### How They Consume Information
- LinkedIn (scrolls, rarely comments)
- Podcasts (while driving/working out, 30-60 min weekly)
- Engineering blogs and conferences (quarterly)
- Direct peer conversations (monthly)
- Email newsletters (if curated and technical)

### What Converts Them
1. **A post that validates their anxiety** ("Commoditization is real, here's the data")
2. **A clear reframe** ("Your moat isn't your tech stack anymore, here's why")
3. **A concrete example** ("Here's what that looks like at OneEleven: X changed when we...")
4. **A peer or thought leader they respect** ("Azeem Azhar also said...")
5. **Actionable next step** ("If this resonates, here's what we explore in the next post: ...")

### Content That Lands
- Data-backed strategic provocations
- Real operational examples (not theory)
- Dry wit (engineers appreciate irreverence)
- Vulnerability where it shows strength (admitting what didn't work)
- Clear thesis with evidence

### Win Condition
Following The Kindness Flywheel, sharing posts in engineering Slack, eventually enrolling in coaching or advisory with you. Bringing your framework to their board conversations.

## **Persona 2: The Strategy-Rethinking Founder**

### Demographics & Context
- **Title:** Founder, CEO (less often: President/COO of founder's company)
- **Age:** 28-42
- **Funding stage:** Series A to Series C (18-36 months in, often $5M-$50M raised)
- **Background:** Technical founder or technical co-founder (not pure business/sales)
- **Team size:** 20-120 people

### The Situation
Built the company on a technical insight, raised money, scaled the team, and now watching competitors build equivalent products with less capital and less time. The original differentiation isn't holding. Either pivoting or doubling down on speed/features, but neither feels right.

### What Keeps Them Up at Night
- **The reckoning:** "What was the moat, actually? Was there ever one? Are we just being out-built?"
- **The fundraising trap:** Investors push for growth at all costs. But growth without differentiation is expensive and fragile.
- **The team crisis:** Attracting talent got easy (growth story), but retention is hard. People join for the technical challenge or the mission, find neither, leave.
- **The burnout spiral:** Running faster to stay ahead, but "ahead of what?" isn't clear. Team feels it. Culture is eroding.
- **The existential question:** "Is this a real business or am I just building features someone will build better/cheaper later?"

### Current Thinking & Solutions
- "We need better engineers, or faster delivery, or a bigger raise, or a new GTM motion."
- Probably doing: reorg (again), hiring for speed, maybe pivoting the product slightly
- Reading: Y Combinator essays, Sequoia capital advice, some product management
- Conversations: Other founders (peers), maybe a coach, definitely investors
- **What they've tried:** Hired faster. Rewarded velocity. Invested in product-market fit experiments. Things got busier but not clearer.

### Key Decision Drivers
1. **Strategic repositioning narrative:** Needs a *reason* to pivot that isn't "we failed." Needs story for team and investors.
2. **Defensibility:** What actually can't be commoditized? If it's not technology, what is it?
3. **Founder identity:** Want to be known as the person who figured something out, not the one who out-executed competitors temporarily.
4. **Team coherence:** Want to give the team something real to believe in, not just "grow faster."

### Objections & Resistance
- **"This sounds like we admit we can't win on tech"** → Reframe as "tech is table stakes, moat is somewhere else"
- **"We don't have time for philosophy"** → Needs to see it as strategy, not added work
- **"Our competitive advantage IS our team"** → "Exactly. Here's how to build a system where the team compounds instead of burns out."
- **"Kindness to who? Customers? Employees? Investors?"** → Needs clarity on what this means operationally
- **"I'm not a 'culture person'"** → Reframe as organizational economics, not culture initiative

### How They Consume Information
- Twitter/X (high volume, real-time, founder takes)
- Podcasts (founder stories and strategy, weekly)
- Direct peer conversations (high frequency, informal)
- Books (on strategy/leadership, 2-3/year)
- Direct messages from thought leaders/peers (very responsive)
- Occasional long-form essays if they hit the right nerve

### What Converts Them
1. **A provocative strategic claim** ("Your tech moat is dead; here's what's defensible")
2. **Data from sources they respect** (McKinsey Jan 2026, MIT Sloan, but also other founders)
3. **An example they can relate to** ("Here's how a fintech reframed this when commoditization hit")
4. **A path forward that's not "be nicer"** ("Here's how organizational trust becomes competitive advantage: [concrete examples]")
5. **Permission to reconsider** ("It's not about moving slower; it's about moving smarter")

### Content That Lands
- Strategic provocations with data
- Founder experience (lived learning, not theory)
- Honest reflection on what failed and why
- Clear competitive repositioning arguments
- Case studies and examples (specific enough to be credible, anonymous enough to protect relationships)

### Win Condition
Following The Kindness Flywheel, resharing posts to their leadership team, eventually working with you as strategic advisor or coach on repositioning. Using your framework in fundraising/team conversations.

## **Persona Comparison Matrix**

| Dimension | The Threatened CTO | The Strategy-Rethinking Founder |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Pain point (primary)** | Commoditization eroding moat, team anxiety rising | Original differentiation failing, need new story |
| **Identity threat** | Technical expertise losing value | Founder/visionary losing clarity |
| **Consumes via** | LinkedIn, podcasts, peer conversations | Twitter, podcasts, direct DMs, peer conversations |
| **Decision speed** | Moderate (needs credibility + peer validation) | Fast (trusts instinct + peer wisdom) |
| **Objection level to "kindness"** | High (too soft) | Moderate (if framed as strategy) |
| **Wants from KF** | Data + path + peer validation | Strategic repositioning + founder story |
| **Conversion signal** | Sharing post in eng Slack, asking follow-up | DM/reply to post, asks for 1:1 |
| **Likely next move** | Coaching; internal org changes | Strategic advisory; maybe coaching later |

## **Content Strategy Implications**

### For the Threatened CTO
- Lead with data + moat language
- Use examples from engineering/ops (retention, async, execution)
- Signal peer validation ("other CTOs are seeing this")
- Make it actionable (concrete changes they can consider)

### For the Strategy-Rethinking Founder
- Lead with strategy/competitive positioning
- Use founder examples (case studies, peer wisdom)
- Give permission to reconsider (it's not failure, it's evolution)
- Signal other founders are thinking this way

### Both
- Emphasize you're a practitioner (not consultant)
- Use dry wit and irreverence
- Show the philosophy underneath, but land in business results
- Make each post shareable within their circles (eng team, leadership team, investors)
