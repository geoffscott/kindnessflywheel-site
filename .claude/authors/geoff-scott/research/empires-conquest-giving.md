*Compiled: 2026-04-18*

## 1. The First Empires and the Rise of "Progress = Accumulation"

### Pre-Empire Human Organization (~300,000 BC – ~3000 BC)

Before empires, humans lived in small-scale societies for the vast majority of our species' history:

- **Hunter-gatherer bands** (most of human history): Generally egalitarian, with leadership based on skill/consensus rather than coercion. Anthropological evidence (bone density studies, burial analysis) suggests relatively equal distribution of labor and resources.
- **Early agricultural settlements** (Neolithic, ~10,000 BC): The shift to farming created surplus, which created the *possibility* of accumulation — but didn't immediately create hierarchy. Some Neolithic societies (Çatalhöyük, ~7500 BC) appear strikingly egalitarian, with no evidence of rulers or palaces.
- **David Graeber's research** argues that early humans moved fluidly between hierarchical and egalitarian arrangements — the "teleological march toward hierarchy" narrative is itself a modern myth.

**Key insight:** Hierarchy and accumulation were not inevitable outcomes of civilization. They were choices — or at least, emergent patterns that could have gone differently.

### The Akkadian Empire: Year Zero for Extraction (~2334 BC)

- **Sargon of Akkad** (~2334–2279 BC) created what most historians consider the first true empire, unifying Sumerian city-states under centralized rule.
- The defining innovation: **conquest as economic strategy**. Take territory → extract tribute → fund armies → take more territory.
- This is the flywheel of empire, and it has been the dominant model of "progress" for ~4,300 years.

### Timeline of Major Empires

| Period | Empire | Defining Pattern |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| ~2334 BC | Akkadian Empire | First empire; centralized conquest and tribute |
| ~1600–1178 BC | Egyptian New Kingdom | Territorial expansion, monumental extraction |
| ~550–330 BC | Persian (Achaemenid) Empire | Sophisticated taxation and administration of conquered peoples |
| ~336–323 BC | Macedonian/Alexander | Conquest as cultural mission ("Hellenization") |
| 27 BC – 476 AD | Roman Empire | Infrastructure as control mechanism; extraction through taxation and slavery |
| 632–750 AD | Umayyad Caliphate | Religious conquest; rapid territorial expansion |
| 1206–1368 | Mongol Empire | Largest contiguous land empire; extreme extraction through tribute |
| 1299–1922 | Ottoman Empire | Religious and territorial expansion across three continents |
| 1500s–1900s | European Colonial Empires | Extraction of resources and labor from colonized peoples; justified by religious and racial ideology |
| 1600s–1947 | British Empire | Largest empire in history (~25% of world's land); industrial-scale extraction |

**Pattern:** Every empire follows the same logic — accumulate wealth and territory through conquest, maintain control through force and administration, justify it through ideology (divine right, civilizing mission, racial superiority, manifest destiny).

### When Did "Progress = Accumulation" Become Dominant?

This is contested, but a reasonable timeline:

1. **~3000 BC**: Surplus agriculture makes accumulation possible
2. **~2334 BC**: Sargon demonstrates that conquest scales
3. **~500 BC – 500 AD**: Greek and Roman philosophy formalize wealth and power as virtues (though Greek philosophy also contains strong counter-traditions — Stoicism, Cynicism)
4. **~1500s AD**: European colonialism globalizes the extraction model
5. **~1776 AD**: Adam Smith's *Wealth of Nations* formalizes accumulation as economic theory
6. **~1900s–present**: GDP becomes the universal measure of national "progress"

## 2. Religious Conquest: Organized Belief as a Mechanism for Elimination

### Christianity's Arc from Persecuted Sect to Imperial Religion

| Date | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| ~30 AD | Death of Jesus | Origin of Christianity as a Jewish sect |
| 64 AD | Nero's persecution | Christians as scapegoats |
| 313 AD | Edict of Milan | Constantine legalizes Christianity |
| 380 AD | Edict of Thessalonica | Christianity becomes sole state religion of Rome |
| 391–392 AD | Theodosius I | Pagan worship banned; temples destroyed or converted |
| 1095–1291 | Crusades | Holy wars for territory; ~1–3 million dead (estimates vary widely) |
| 1184–1834 | Inquisition(s) | Systematic elimination of heresy; Spanish Inquisition ~3,000–5,000 executed, broader impact through terror and forced conversion |
| 1492–1900s | Colonial missions | Christianity as justification for colonization; Doctrine of Discovery (1493) gave Christian nations right to claim non-Christian lands |

### Islamic Expansion

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 632–661 | Rashidun Caliphate — rapid expansion after Muhammad's death |
| 661–750 | Umayyad Caliphate — from Spain to Central Asia |
| 750–1258 | Abbasid Caliphate — cultural golden age, but maintained through military power |

### Indigenous Belief Systems Systematically Destroyed

- **European paganism**: Roman/Celtic/Norse/Germanic traditions systematically eliminated 4th–12th centuries
- **Indigenous American spirituality**: Suppressed through mission systems, residential schools, explicit bans on ceremony (US banned Native religious practices until 1978 — the American Indian Religious Freedom Act)
- **African traditional religions**: Disrupted through slave trade and colonial missionary activity
- **Australian Aboriginal spirituality**: Disrupted through forced removal (Stolen Generations)
- **Pre-Islamic Arabian, Persian, and Central Asian traditions**: Largely replaced

### The Body Count (Rough Historical Estimates)

These numbers are contested and imprecise, but the scale matters:

- **Crusades**: 1–3 million dead
- **Inquisitions**: Thousands executed directly; broader cultural terror affected millions
- **European wars of religion** (1524–1648): 5–12 million dead
- **Colonial genocide of Indigenous peoples in the Americas**: Estimated 90% population decline (from ~60 million to ~6 million) — primarily from disease, but also direct violence, forced labor, and deliberate destruction of food systems
- **Atlantic slave trade**: ~12.5 million enslaved; ~2 million died in transit
- **Congo Free State** (Leopold II): ~10 million dead

**Important caveat:** Not all of these deaths were "religious" in motivation — economic extraction, racial ideology, and political power were always intertwined. Religion provided the *justification framework*, not always the primary motive.

## 3. The Alternative: Cultures That Defined Success as Giving

### Gift Economies and Redistribution

**Potlatch (Pacific Northwest Indigenous peoples)**
- Among the Kwakwaka'wakw, Haida, Tlingit, and other peoples, **the wealthiest person was the one who gave away the most**.
- Potlatch ceremonies involved massive redistribution of wealth — blankets, food, coppers (prestige items).
- Status came from generosity, not accumulation. A chief who hoarded was seen as weak.
- The Canadian government **banned potlatch from 1884 to 1951** — a direct attempt to impose the accumulation model on peoples who rejected it.

**Important nuance:** Potlatch was also competitive — chiefs competed to *out-give* each other. It wasn't a utopia; it was a different value system with its own power dynamics.

### Ubuntu Philosophy (Southern Africa)

- **"Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu"** — "A person is a person through other people"
- Emphasizes interconnectedness, communal well-being, and relational identity
- Not just philosophy — embedded in traditional governance, conflict resolution (restorative justice), and economic practices
- Desmond Tutu and Nelson Mandela both drew heavily on Ubuntu in post-apartheid reconciliation
- Modern application: communal entrepreneurship, cooperative economics

### Buddhist Economics

- **E.F. Schumacher** coined the term in 1966 (essay "Buddhist Economics," later expanded in *Small Is Beautiful*, 1973)
- Core principle: **"Right livelihood"** — work should serve human well-being, not just produce wealth
- Challenges the assumption that more consumption = more welfare
- Influenced by actual Buddhist practice: the Middle Way, non-attachment, sufficiency

### Bhutan's Gross National Happiness (GNH)

- Introduced by King Jigme Singye Wangchuck in 1972 as an alternative to GDP
- Four pillars: sustainable development, environmental conservation, cultural preservation, good governance
- Nine domains measured: living standards, health, education, governance, ecological diversity, time use, psychological well-being, community vitality, cultural resilience
- **Radical premise:** national success is measured by citizens' well-being, not economic output

### Other Alternative Frameworks

- **Effective Altruism**: Modern philosophical movement focused on maximizing good done (though critics note it can become its own form of optimization/accumulation logic)
- **B Corporations**: Companies certified to balance profit with purpose (~8,000+ certified as of 2025)
- **Doughnut Economics** (Kate Raworth): Economic model that aims to meet human needs without overshooting planetary boundaries
- **Buen Vivir** (Andean/Latin American): "Good living" — harmony with nature and community as the measure of a good life; enshrined in Ecuador's and Bolivia's constitutions

## 4. The Honest Counterargument: What Empires Actually Produced

It's intellectually dishonest to pretend empires produced nothing of value:

- **Infrastructure**: Roman roads, aqueducts, and legal systems; Persian postal systems; British railways (even if built through exploitation)
- **Technology**: Military competition drove innovation in metallurgy, engineering, navigation, medicine
- **Knowledge preservation**: Islamic Golden Age preserved and expanded Greek/Roman learning; Mongol Empire facilitated trade and knowledge exchange across Eurasia
- **Scale**: Empires enabled coordination at a scale that small communities couldn't — large irrigation projects, standardized trade, shared languages
- **Modern medicine, science, and technology** emerged from societies organized around the accumulation model

**The question isn't whether empires produced results. They did. The question is whether the extraction model was the *only* way to produce those results — and whether the costs (genocide, slavery, ecological destruction, spiritual devastation) were worth it.**

## 5. The Modern Inflection Point

### Why the Extraction Model Is Running Out of Runway

- **Ecological limits**: Climate change, biodiversity loss, resource depletion — the planet cannot sustain infinite extraction
- **Social limits**: Rising inequality, mental health crises, loneliness epidemics — the accumulation model isn't producing well-being even for the "winners"
- **AI and automation**: When machines can do most productive work, what is "progress"? The extraction model has no answer for a post-scarcity economy
- **Trust collapse**: Institutions built on extraction (corporations, governments, religions) face declining trust globally

### The Business Case for the Alternative

- Companies with high employee well-being outperform peers (Gallup data consistently shows this)
- Customer loyalty driven by genuine care outperforms loyalty driven by switching costs (the Kindness Flywheel thesis)
- The most valuable modern companies (in terms of longevity and resilience) tend to have strong mission alignment, not just profit motive
