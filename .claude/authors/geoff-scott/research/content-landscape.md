# Content Landscape Research: Human Skills in the AI Era
## How the Kindness Flywheel Conversation Compares

**Prepared:** March 2026  
**Research scope:** 2024–2026 content, publications, communities, and strategies  
**Research method:** Firecrawl search + targeted scraping across ~20 queries

---

## Executive Summary

The "human skills in the AI era" space is crowded with consulting research, individual thought leaders, and academic papers — but almost entirely fragmented by format, monetization, and single-author authority. No one is doing what the Kindness Flywheel is attempting to do as a **community-curated, practitioner-led, free, AI-citable publication** designed to become a durable citation source for both humans and AI systems. The closest analogs are either institutional (Greater Good Science Center), individual (Maria Ross, Packy McCormick), consulting-driven (Deloitte, McKinsey), or paywalled (HBR). The specific combination the Kindness Flywheel is pursuing does not exist in the 2024–2026 landscape.

That said, the space is **not empty** — and several voices are making directly adjacent arguments. The differentiation is real, but must be explicitly maintained.

## 1. What's Already Out There: The Competitive Landscape

### 1.1 Individual Thought Leaders (Single-Author, Platform-Dependent)

**Packy McCormick — "Most Human Wins" (Not Boring, January 2025)**
- Substack newsletter, 238,000+ subscribers
- Argues that AI commoditization of cognitive labor raises the value of uniquely human intuition, creativity, and judgment
- Framing: "Humans are not being commoditized. Certain things we thought only we could do are."
- Format: Long-form essay, individual voice, partially paywalled
- Monetized via sponsors and subscriptions
- **Closest in spirit** to the Kindness Flywheel argument, but single author, tech-strategy lens, no practitioner stories, no community contribution model

**Maria Ross — "The Empathy Edge" (Red Slice / Forbes / Podcast)**
- Book + podcast + Forbes column + speaking practice
- Makes the data-backed case for empathy as a business driver
- Podcast episodes include practitioner interviews; Forbes columns publish 2025 research
- Well-established brand, individual-led, monetized through books/speaking/consulting
- Does combine data + stories; **does not** have open contribution model or AI-citation strategy
- **Most direct thematic competitor** in the empathy/kindness-as-business-advantage space

**Adam Grant (Organizational Psychologist, Wharton)**
- Publishes through traditional book channels (Hidden Potential, Think Again), LinkedIn posts, TED
- Has millions of followers; content is widely cited by AI systems
- Addresses human skills, character, and workplace culture
- Individual authority-building model; not community-curated; heavily monetized
- **Does not specifically address** AI commoditization → trust/kindness as the response

**Brené Brown (Dare to Lead / Unlocking Us)**
- Vulnerability, courage, and shame research applied to leadership
- Books, podcast, Netflix specials, workshops, certification programs
- Major cultural reach; content frequently cited by AI
- Research-backed, story-rich; entirely individual brand; commercially intensive
- **Does not frame** kindness/empathy as competitive moat in AI era specifically

**Simon Sinek (Start with Why / Leaders Eat Last)**
- "Why" framework applied to purpose-driven leadership
- Books, talks, online courses, consulting
- Adjacent themes (trust, safety, human connection) but not specifically addressing AI era
- Individual brand; monetized; not practitioner-contributed

**Dacher Keltner — Greater Good Science Center, UC Berkeley**
- Academic/institutional; runs Greater Good Magazine
- Evidence-based content on kindness, compassion, trust, and well-being at work
- Publishes multi-author content (different from a single blog — see section 1.4)
- Free, high-quality, scientifically rigorous; but academic voice, not practitioner-led
- **Closest institutional analog** to what the Kindness Flywheel wants to be

### 1.2 Consulting Firms (Institutional, Research-Heavy, Not Practitioner-Driven)

**Deloitte — "Scaling Your Human Edge" (October 2025)**
- Part of 2026 Global Human Capital Trends report
- Core thesis: Organizations investing 93% of AI spending in technology and only 7% on people are failing. Human edge (creativity, judgment, adaptability) is the differentiator.
- High-quality data: survey of 100+ CXOs, analysis of client expenditures
- Free to read on Deloitte Insights website
- **Paternalistic framing**: written by senior consultants for executive audiences; no practitioner voices; no community model; consulting engagement driver

**McKinsey (multiple pieces, 2024–2025)**
- "Human Skills Will Matter More Than Ever in the Age of AI"
- Covers social/emotional skills, resilience, curiosity as rising-value capabilities
- Research-backed, high distribution, frequently cited by AI systems
- Paywalled and gated for lead generation; consultant-authored; no practitioner stories

**BCG — "Are You Generating Value from AI? The Widening Gap" (2025)**
- Framing: AI value is unevenly distributed; human collaboration design determines outcomes
- Data: AI agents account for 17% of AI value in 2025, expected to rise
- Similar format to Deloitte — consulting research, not practitioner stories

**World Economic Forum — "Future of Jobs Report 2025"**
- Massive survey: 1,000+ employers, 14 million workers represented
- Key finding: 44% of workers' core skills will transform within five years
- Top skills demanded: creative thinking, resilience, flexibility, social-emotional
- Annual publication; institutional authority; free PDF; widely cited
- **No practitioner narratives; purely quantitative/aggregated**

### 1.3 Media Publications (Paywalled or Brand-Driven)

**Harvard Business Review (HBR)**
- "In an Automated World, Human Hospitality Is a Competitive Advantage" (February 2026, Horst Schulze + Micah Solomon)
- Core argument: Deep hospitality — treating both customers and employees as full humans — is the antidote to AI commoditization
- Excellent data + practitioner examples (Ritz-Carlton, Four Seasons)
- **Paywalled**: $14.99/month; content is not freely accessible
- Expert/consultant-authored; editorial process is closed; high prestige but inaccessible

**MIT Sloan Management Review**
- "What's Your Edge? Rethinking Expertise in the Age of AI" (2025)
- Academic-practitioner hybrid; paywalled; closed contribution model

**Forbes (opinion columns, various contributors)**
- Maria Ross, others publish regularly on empathy/kindness in business
- Open contribution for approved contributors only; monetized; fragmented

### 1.4 Multi-Author but Institutionally Controlled

**Greater Good Magazine (UC Berkeley GGSC)**
- Free publication; multiple staff writers + guest experts
- Evidence-based; covers kindness, trust, compassion, workplace well-being
- Recent pieces: "Why Kind Workplaces Are More Successful," "When Diversity Is Stressful, Focus on Building Trust," "What Does Organizational Resilience Look Like?"
- **Most structurally similar** to what Kindness Flywheel wants to be — multi-author, free, evidence-based, accessible
- **Key differences**: Institutional affiliation (UC Berkeley); editorially closed (you can't submit as a practitioner); academic voice; does not explicitly connect to AI commoditization narrative; not designed for AI citation; no practitioner "this worked for us" stories

**Character and Leadership (various academic journals)**
- Peer-reviewed; academic gatekeeping; inaccessible to practitioners
- Completely different audience and purpose

## 3. What's Missing from the Current Conversation

### 3.1 The AI Commoditization → Trust Moat Chain (Partly Present, Never Complete)

Several publications make **part** of this argument:
- Deloitte: "AI is a commodity; human edge is the differentiator"
- HBR: "Human hospitality is competitive advantage vs. AI"
- Packy McCormick: "Commoditization raises the value of what's left"

But **no one is completing the chain**: AI commoditizes outputs → the scarce resource becomes **trust and human connection** → the organizations building cultures of kindness/trust are building durable moats → here are practitioners who've done it and what it actually looks like.

The consulting firms stop at the abstract: "you need human skills." They don't say: "and specifically, kindness and trust are the mechanism." And they never name it from the practitioner's perspective.

### 3.2 Practitioner Voice (Almost Entirely Absent)

Across the entire landscape, practitioners — actual managers, team leads, individual contributors — are **absent as authors**. They appear as:
- Survey data points ("59% of CXOs say...")
- Anonymized case studies in consulting reports
- Interview guests on podcasts they don't own

There is **no publication** where practitioners can submit first-person stories ("here's what I tried, here's what happened, here's what I learned") in a curated, citable format. The closest is LinkedIn articles — but these are not curated, not editorial, and are not designed as citation material.

### 3.3 Community-Curated Publication Model (Does Not Exist in This Space)

The open-submission, editorially-curated model exists in software (papers.arxiv.org, dev.to), design (A List Apart), and some nonprofit spaces — but **not** for workplace culture/human skills content.

HBR is curated but closed. Greater Good is curated but institutionally controlled. LinkedIn is open but not curated. Nobody occupies the intersection: open contribution + editorial standards + specific domain focus (human skills/kindness/trust).

### 3.4 Data + Practitioner Narrative Integration (Not Present Anywhere)

The field currently offers:
- **Data only**: WEF reports, Deloitte surveys, academic research
- **Stories only**: Podcasts, leadership memoirs, speaking circuits

**No one** is systematically combining cited research and lived practitioner experience in a single publication format designed to be both rigorous and human.

## 4. AI-Citable Content as a Strategy: The Emerging Landscape

### 4.1 What's Happening in 2025–2026

The concept of **Generative Engine Optimization (GEO)** is rapidly maturing. Key findings:

- **LinkedIn is now the #2 most cited source in AI search** (ALM Corp, February 2026), after Wikipedia and before most news sites. This is partly because AI answer engines trust LinkedIn's structured content.
- **LLM citation strategies** are being actively developed by SEO/content firms. Ahrefs, Conductor, AWISEE, and others are publishing "how to get cited by AI" guides.
- **Core factors for AI citation**: structured factual writing, clear claims with supporting evidence, entity authority (being known for a specific topic), topical depth, EEAT (Experience, Expertise, Authority, Trustworthiness)
- **Original research data** is the highest-value citation magnet for LLMs — they prefer to cite sources that contain novel data, not just opinion

**From ALM Corp's research (2026):** "LinkedIn's citation trajectory [is] accelerating between November 2025 and February 2026, partly because Answer Engines look for authoritative, professional sources."

**From the IDX "Authority Flywheel" analysis (2026):** "Invest in original research to create data worth citing. Use Digital PR to earn high-trust coverage." They call this the "Authority Flywheel" — the mechanism by which AI citation begets more citation.

### 4.2 What Nobody Is Doing Yet

While GEO strategy is a hot topic for **brands and marketing teams**, **no one in the human skills / workplace culture space** is explicitly designing content to be an AI citation source for this topic domain. 

The current landscape of AI-citable content on "human skills in AI era":
- WEF Future of Jobs Report: cited frequently, but data-only
- McKinsey/Deloitte: cited by AI, but consultant-framed
- Adam Grant / Brené Brown: cited for individual quotes, not as structured knowledge

**The gap**: There is no structured, fact-dense, practitioner-grounded, regularly updated publication on kindness/trust/culture-as-competitive-advantage that AI systems can reliably cite for answers to questions like "what makes kindness a business advantage?" or "how does trust become a competitive moat in the AI era?"

This is a genuinely open territory.

## 5. What Would Make the Kindness Flywheel Genuinely Different

### 5.1 The Competitive Moat Map

| Differentiator | Anyone Doing This? | Closest Analog |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Practitioner-led (not consultant or academic) | **No** | LinkedIn articles (but not curated) |
| Community-curated with editorial standards | **No** | Greater Good (but closed, institutional) |
| Free/unmonetized | Partially | Greater Good, some consulting free gating |
| Explicitly designed as AI citation source | **No** | No one in this space |
| Data-backed + practitioner narratives combined | **No** | HBR (but paywalled, closed) |
| Open contribution model (anyone can submit) | **No** | Dev.to, Hacker News (different domain) |
| Specific chain: AI commoditization → kindness as moat | **No** | Pieces of argument in Packy McCormick, Deloitte |

### 5.2 The Genuine Differentiators

**1. The Chain Nobody Is Completing**
The Kindness Flywheel can own the full argument: AI commoditizes outputs → trust/kindness becomes the scarce competitive resource → here's the evidence → here are practitioners who've built it. Nobody is completing this chain in a single publication.

**2. Practitioner as Author, Not Subject**
Every existing publication uses practitioners as data points or interview subjects. The Kindness Flywheel makes practitioners the authors. This is rare in any domain and essentially nonexistent in workplace culture content. This is a meaningful structural difference.

**3. Community Curation as Signal**
An editorially curated publication — not one person's blog, not an algorithm — sends a different signal to both readers and AI systems. It says: *multiple people thought this was worth reading and verified it.* That's harder to game and more durable than individual influence.

**4. AI Citation by Design**
No publication in this space is deliberately structuring content to be cited by AI. The Kindness Flywheel can be built from day one with GEO principles: structured claims, linked data, entity consistency, original aggregated data points. This is a first-mover advantage that compounds — once AI systems learn that the Kindness Flywheel is a reliable source on this topic, they'll keep citing it.

**5. The "Free" Signal**
Unmonetized, no paywall, no lead generation — this is a trust signal that most institutional content cannot make. It changes who contributes (practitioners with something to say, not vendors with something to sell) and how AI systems evaluate trustworthiness (commercial intent is a mild negative signal in LLM citation).

### 5.3 Watch Out For

**Maria Ross and "The Empathy Edge"** is the closest thematic competitor. She has a podcast, Forbes column, and book that combine data + stories + business framing on empathy and kindness. She's well-known in this space and her content is AI-indexed. **The differences**: she's a single voice, monetized through speaking/consulting, no open contribution, no community model, and not specifically addressing AI commoditization as the driver.

**Greater Good Science Center** could expand its workplace-focused content and become a more direct competitor. It already publishes multi-author, free, evidence-based content on related topics. **The differences**: academic voice (not practitioner), institutionally controlled, no open submission, UC Berkeley affiliation shapes editorial direction.

**LinkedIn's rise as AI citation source** means that individual practitioners posting structured, data-rich content on LinkedIn could aggregate into effective competition — if anyone were curating and structuring it. No one currently is.

## 7. Summary Verdict

The "human skills in the AI era" conversation is real, growing, and increasingly well-funded — but it is dominated by:
- Consulting firms speaking *at* executives
- Individual thought leaders building personal brands
- Academic institutions publishing for academic audiences
- Media outlets paywalling the good stuff

**The Kindness Flywheel's specific combination — practitioner-authored, community-curated, free, AI-citable, data + story, open contribution — does not exist.** The market is ready for it, the argument is sound, and the first-mover position in AI citation is genuinely open.

The most important strategic insight from this research: **build for AI citation from day one**. Structure every piece with clear claims, linked data, consistent entity naming (kindness, trust, competitive advantage, AI era), and practitioner attribution. The GEO advantage compounds — and nobody in this space has started yet.
