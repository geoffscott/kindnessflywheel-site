# Fact-Check: Greg Reynolds' "Coordination Tax" ($1.6T–$7T)

**Date:** 2026-03-29  
**Status:** Complete  
**Verdict:** The $1.6T–$7T range is **directionally plausible but methodologically weak**. A defensible, peer-review-ready number is harder to pin down than Greg suggests.

---

## 1. What Greg Actually Claims

*Background on Greg in [`../people/greg-reynolds.md`](../people/greg-reynolds.md).*

Greg uses several different numbers across his writings, which is itself a red flag:

| Article | Claimed Cost | What He's Measuring |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| LinkedIn post (2026) | **$1.6 trillion** "coordination tax" | Collaboration/coordination failures |
| LinkedIn "$4.8B Aspiration" | **$7 trillion** "Coordination Tax" | Same concept, larger scope |
| Medium "Grid Isn't Broken" | **$7 trillion** Coordination Tax | Same |
| Medium "$12 Trillion" article | **$12 trillion** "hidden waste" | Broader: all systemic inefficiency |
| Medium "$305 Trillion Glitch" | **$305 trillion** "Collaboration Tax" | Equates global debt with coordination failure |

**Critical finding:** Greg's numbers range from $1.6T to $305T depending on the article. The $305T figure equates *total global debt* with coordination failure, which is nonsensical — debt is not a "cost" in the same sense. The $12T figure is vague. The $1.6T–$7T range is the most restrained claim.

### Greg's Cited Sources

Greg cites **zero primary sources** in the articles reviewed. He:
- References Oliver Williamson's transaction cost economics (conceptually, not a specific number)
- Uses his own coined terms (CLI, CEP, Coordination Alpha) without empirical grounding
- Provides no methodology for how $1.6T, $7T, or $12T were calculated
- Never links to Gallup, McKinsey, or any research organization

**This is the fundamental problem: there are no sources to trace back.**

## 2. Independent Estimate: What DO We Know?

### Category A: Employee Disengagement (VERIFIED — Strong Source)

**Gallup State of the Global Workplace Report (2023–2024)**
- **$8.8–$8.9 trillion** annually in lost productivity from disengaged employees
- **~9% of global GDP**
- Methodology: Gallup surveys 2.2M+ employees across 160+ countries. Applies proprietary formula: engagement level × salary × productivity differential. Three-step process described on their methodology page.
- **Caveats:** "Lost productivity" is an opportunity cost, not cash out the door. It measures the gap between current engagement and full engagement — a theoretical maximum no economy has achieved.
- **Relevance to "coordination tax":** Partial overlap. Disengagement has many causes (bad management, burnout, compensation) — only some are "coordination" or "trust" failures.

### Category B: Employee Turnover (VERIFIED — Strong Source)

**Gallup (2019), BLS data**
- **$1 trillion/year** in U.S. voluntary turnover costs
- Replacement cost = 0.5–2× annual salary per employee
- Some portion is trust/culture-driven, but people also leave for money, relocation, career growth

### Category C: Organizational Misalignment & Failed Projects (MIXED SOURCES)

- **$2 trillion** in failed initiatives globally (Meahana.io, citing PMI and Standish Group data)
- **$1.2 trillion** in poor workplace communication costs (Grammarly/Harris Poll, extrapolated)
- **$1–1.1 trillion** from employee-role misfit (various HR analytics firms)
- **Quality:** These are mostly extrapolations from survey data applied to global workforce. Reasonable order-of-magnitude but not rigorous.

### Category D: Supply Chain Coordination Failures (VERIFIED — Mixed Sources)

- **$1.3–$1.6 trillion** in supply chain inefficiencies (various industry reports)
- Includes expired goods, logistics waste, inventory mismanagement
- Partially a coordination/trust problem, partially just operational complexity

### Category E: Fraud, Compliance, and Transaction Costs of Low Trust (VERIFIED)

- **$4.4 trillion** in global financial crime (2024 report)
- **$1 trillion+** in global scam losses (SAS, 2024)
- **$5.1 trillion** estimated gross fraud value (ACFE methodology, 5% of revenue)
- Compliance/legal costs are enormous but harder to isolate — estimated at 5–10% of revenue for regulated industries
- **Cybercrime:** $10.5 trillion projected by 2025 (Cybersecurity Ventures — widely cited but methodology questioned)

### Category F: Trust Deficit Macro Estimates

- **Cryptoeconomics (Medium):** Extrapolates ~$29 trillion "cost of trust" globally using transaction cost as % of GDP
- **Deloitte:** Qualitative — trust raises GDP growth but doesn't quantify the gap
- **Stanford GSB:** $2.2 trillion in bank losses from trust erosion (narrow: banking sector only)

## 3. Assessment

### The Aggregation Problem

If you naively add up all the categories:

| Category | Estimate |
|----------|----------|
| Employee disengagement | $8.8T |
| Employee turnover (US only) | $1.0T |
| Misalignment/failed projects | $2.0T |
| Communication failures | $1.2T |
| Supply chain inefficiency | $1.5T |
| Fraud/financial crime | $4.4T |
| **Naive total** | **~$19T** |

But this is **riddled with double-counting**:
- Disengaged employees contribute to failed projects, turnover, AND communication failures — you can't add them all
- Supply chain inefficiency overlaps with organizational misalignment
- Fraud costs overlap with compliance costs
- "Lost productivity" and "failed initiatives" share the same underlying workforce hours

### What a Skeptical Economist Would Say

1. **"Opportunity cost ≠ actual cost."** The Gallup $8.8T is the gap between current productivity and theoretical maximum. No economy has ever achieved full engagement. This is like saying "the cost of not being perfect is $X."

2. **"You can't add categories that share the same underlying cause."** If disengagement causes both turnover ($1T) and low productivity ($8.8T), you can't sum them — the turnover IS part of the productivity loss.

3. **"Global estimates from U.S. survey data are suspect."** Most of these numbers start with U.S. surveys and multiply by global workforce ratios. Labor markets in India, China, and Africa work very differently.

4. **"What's the counterfactual?"** Some coordination costs are the *optimal* level of friction. Compliance exists because fraud exists. Legal costs exist because contracts need enforcement. Zero coordination cost would require perfect trust, which doesn't exist.

5. **"The framing is unfalsifiable."** If you define "coordination tax" broadly enough (any waste, friction, or suboptimality), you can make the number arbitrarily large. At $305T, Greg has essentially done this.

### Is $1.6T–$7T Defensible?

**$1.6 trillion — YES, easily defensible.** This is conservative. U.S. employee turnover alone is $1T. Add supply chain waste and you're there. This is ~1.5% of global GDP, which is a very modest claim.

**$7 trillion — PLAUSIBLE but requires careful scoping.** This is ~6.4% of global GDP. You can get here by taking a subset of the Gallup disengagement number (say, the portion attributable specifically to trust/coordination failures rather than all causes of disengagement) plus supply chain waste plus a portion of fraud/compliance costs. But it requires explicit methodology that Greg doesn't provide.

**Above $7T — increasingly speculative.** $12T requires including categories that go beyond "coordination" into general economic inefficiency. $305T is not a serious number.

### The Defensible Number

**A peer-review-ready version of this claim:**

> "The economic cost of coordination failures — including trust deficits, organizational misalignment, and preventable friction in supply chains and institutions — likely falls in the range of **$2–5 trillion per year globally**, representing **2–5% of global GDP**. This estimate draws on Gallup's employee engagement research (attributing ~30–40% of the $8.8T disengagement cost to coordination/trust factors = $2.6–3.5T), supply chain inefficiency studies ($1.3–1.6T, partially overlapping), and is consistent with transaction cost economics literature. The estimate is inherently imprecise due to definitional ambiguity, double-counting risks, and the difficulty of separating coordination costs from other sources of economic friction."

## 4. Context Check

| Metric | Value | % of Global GDP ($110T) |
|--------|-------|------------------------|
| Greg's low estimate | $1.6T | 1.5% |
| Greg's high estimate | $7T | 6.4% |
| Gallup disengagement (total) | $8.8T | 8.0% |
| Our defensible estimate | $2–5T | 2–5% |

- **1.5% of GDP** for coordination costs is very conservative and almost certainly an undercount
- **6.4% of GDP** is plausible but at the high end of what's supportable without serious methodology
- **8–9% of GDP** (Gallup's full number) includes many non-coordination causes of disengagement
- For comparison: global military spending is ~$2.4T (2.2% GDP); global healthcare waste is estimated at 20–40% of ~$9T spend

### Recommendation for Geoff

If you want to use the "coordination tax" concept in Kindness Flywheel materials:

1. **Use $2–5 trillion** as the range, with explicit sourcing (Gallup, supply chain studies)
2. **Never cite Greg's numbers directly** — they have no traceable methodology
3. **Always caveat** the double-counting risk and opportunity-cost-vs-real-cost distinction
4. **The strongest framing:** "Gallup estimates that employee disengagement alone costs $8.8T/year globally. Even if only a quarter of that is attributable to trust and coordination failures, we're looking at $2+ trillion in preventable waste."
5. **Greg's concept is directionally correct** — the insight that coordination failures are an enormous economic drag is well-supported. His specific numbers and framing are not rigorous enough to cite.
