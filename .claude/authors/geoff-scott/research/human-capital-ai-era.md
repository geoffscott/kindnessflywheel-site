# Research Brief: Human Capital in the AI Era (2024–2026)

**Prepared:** March 28, 2026  
**Scope:** 2024–2026 data only. Older sources flagged with ⚠️  
**Purpose:** Evidence base for The Kindness Flywheel / Growth Science  
**Research method:** Firecrawl search + targeted scrape of primary sources

---

## Executive Summary

The evidence across major consulting, academic, and workforce surveys (2024–2026) converges on a clear pattern: **AI adoption is elevating, not replacing, the value of human skills**. Organizations that succeed with AI are those with high trust, strong leadership support, and cultures of psychological safety. Simultaneously, employee anxiety about AI displacement is high and rising—representing both a human challenge and a strategic opportunity. The data is strongest on skills trends and weakest on direct causal links between specific human qualities (e.g., empathy) and organizational AI outcomes.

### 1. Is AI Adoption Shifting What Organizations Value in People?

**Finding: Yes, with strong convergent evidence across multiple sources.**

#### WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025
*Source: World Economic Forum, "The Future of Jobs Report 2025," weforum.org*

- Employers expect **39% of workers' core skills to change by 2030**
- The **top 5 core skills of 2025** are all cognitive, creative, or social-emotional:
  1. Analytical thinking
  2. Resilience, flexibility, and agility
  3. Leadership and social influence
  4. Creative thinking
  5. Motivation and self-awareness
- Fastest-growing skills over next five years (net % increase in demand):
  - Creative thinking: **+66%**
  - Resilience, flexibility, and agility: **+66%**
  - Leadership and social influence: **+58%**
  - Analytical thinking: **+55%**
- Skills *declining* in demand: manual dexterity, basic programming

#### Workday "Elevating Human Potential: The AI Skills Revolution" (January 2025)
*Source: Workday/Hanover Research, global survey of 2,500 full-time workers across 22 countries, fielded Nov–Dec 2024. newsroom.workday.com*

- **83% of respondents** believe AI will elevate the importance of uniquely human skills and enhance human creativity
- **81%** recognize AI is changing the skills required for their jobs
- Skills deemed **least likely to be replaced by AI** and **most valuable at work**:
  - Ethical decision-making
  - Connection and relationship building
  - Emotional intelligence and empathy
  - Conflict resolution
- **93% of active AI users** agree AI allows them to focus on higher-level responsibilities (strategy, problem-solving)
- Notable disconnect: **82% of individual contributors** believe human connection will intensify in need as AI grows, but only **65% of managers** agree — a 17-point gap

#### SHRM "Why Human-Centered Skills Are the Next Hiring Frontier" (June 2025)
*Source: SHRM.org, citing SHRM 2025 Talent Trends Report and WEF data*

- More than **1 in 4 organizations** say full-time positions hired for in the past year required new skills
- More than **2 in 3 organizations** report difficulty finding qualified candidates
- **55% of U.S. employers** say determining whether candidates have the right soft skills is the *most difficult* part of hiring
- Quote (Alex Alonso, SHRM Chief Data & Analytics Officer): *"The transition from role-based to capabilities-based hiring is more than a passing trend; it reflects an essential shift toward building resilient, future-focused teams."*

#### HBR "Soft Skills Matter Now More Than Ever" (August 2025)
*Source: Hosseinioun, Neffke, Youn & Zhang, HBR.org, Aug 26, 2025. Based on analysis of 1,000+ occupations and 70 million job transitions (2005–2019 data ⚠️ but published 2025)*

- Workers with strong foundational skills (reading comprehension, math, communication) earn higher wages, move into advanced roles faster, and are more resilient to industry changes
- **Social skills** (communication, empathy, collaboration) are the subset of foundational skills most correlated with highest professional attainment
- Since 2007, employers have **tripled** the share of managerial job postings emphasizing collaboration, coaching, and influence — while language about traditional supervision declined
- The "half-life" of technical skills has dropped from ~10 years in 1980s to **4 years today**, heading toward 2 years — making adaptable foundational skills more critical
- ⚠️ Note: The underlying longitudinal dataset is 2005–2019; the paper and HBR article were published 2025

#### McKinsey "Superagency in the Workplace" (January 2025)
*Source: McKinsey, Jan 28, 2025. Description only — site blocks scraping*

- **46% of leaders** identify skill gaps in their workforces as a significant barrier to AI adoption
- Characterized as covering how Gen AI is reshaping work dynamics, productivity, and leadership alignment

### 2. Trust and Psychological Safety as Predictors of AI Adoption Success

**Finding: Emerging strong signal, not yet deeply quantified — but multiple credible sources point the same direction.**

#### BCG "AI at Work 2025: Momentum Builds, but Gaps Remain" (June 2025)
*Source: BCG, global survey of 10,600+ leaders, managers, and frontline employees across 11 countries, June 26, 2025. bcg.com*

- Frontline AI adoption has **stalled at 51%** regular use — the "silicon ceiling"
- **The share of employees who feel positive about GenAI rises from 15% to 55% with strong leadership support** — a 3.7x increase
- Only ~**one-quarter of frontline employees** say they receive strong leadership support for AI
- When employees lack the AI tools they need, **more than half** find workarounds ("shadow AI") anyway — creating security risks and fragmentation
- Only **one-third** of employees say they have been properly trained on AI

#### PwC "Global Workforce Hopes and Fears Survey 2025" (November 2025)
*Source: PwC, ~50,000 respondents across 28 sectors in 48 economies, Nov 12, 2025. pwc.com*

- **54% of workers** across all industries have used AI in the last 12 months; but only **14% use GenAI daily**
- Workers who use GenAI daily are far more likely to report AI improving their:
  - Productivity: **90%** (daily users) vs. **59%** (infrequent users)
  - Work quality: **90%** vs. **62%**
  - Job security: **58%** vs. **32%**
- Workers are **twice as likely to be curious or excited** about AI's impact as to be worried or confused
- PwC explicitly states: *"leaders should try to co-create the future with their people"* and calls out **trust, psychological safety, and meaningful work** as key motivators unlocking AI adoption
- **Six key actions** PwC recommends all center on human factors: acknowledging uncertainty, addressing trust gaps, inspiring with clear vision, nurturing skills, offering meaningful work, strategic alignment

#### Psychology Today / Jonathan Brill "Psychological Safety Drives AI Adoption" (September 2025)
*Source: PsychologyToday.com, Jonathan Brill, posted Sept 22, 2025*

- Directly cites Amy Edmondson's Harvard Business School research: **"team learning requires psychological safety"**
- AI adoption adds an "existential twist" to psychological threat: not just social embarrassment, but professional survival
- Studies on proactivity show people take initiative when **both autonomous and psychologically safe** — most AI rollouts violate both conditions
- IMF estimate cited: AI will **negatively impact 30% of jobs in advanced economies**, escalating workplace anxiety
- Proposes "Neural Necklace" — four practical tools for building psychological safety within AI adoption contexts (Risk Bands, Three-Lens Challenge, Magnitude Mapping, 48-Hour Rule)
- The article treats psychological safety as a *precondition* for AI adoption, not a nice-to-have

#### MIT Technology Review (December 2025)

- URL: `https://www.technologyreview.com/2025/12/16/1125899/creating-psychological-safety-in-the-ai-era/`
- Title: "Creating Psychological Safety in the AI Era"

*Source: Deloitte press release, deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/high-performing-teams.html, January 2026*

### 3. Employee Experience in AI-Heavy Organizations: Engagement, Burnout, Turnover

**Finding: Deeply split picture — AI exposure correlates with both risk and protection from burnout, depending on how it's deployed.**

#### Gallup "State of the Global Workplace 2025"
*Source: Gallup, annual global study, gallup.com*

- **Global employee engagement fell from 23% to 21% in 2024** — only the second decline in 12 years (the first was COVID-19 lockdowns in 2020)
- Disengagement cost the global economy **$438 billion** in lost productivity in 2024
- **Manager engagement fell from 30% to 27%** — female manager engagement dropped **7 points**, young manager (under 35) engagement fell **5 points**
- **70% of team engagement is attributable to the manager** — manager breakdown is the primary cause of the broader decline
- **33% of the world's employees say they are thriving** in their lives overall (down from peak of 35% in 2022)
- Gallup links AI adoption challenges explicitly to the manager pressure collapse: *"new systems"* cited by managers as a key stressor
- **$9.6 trillion** in potential productivity could be added if the global workforce was fully engaged

#### UKG/Workplace Intelligence Global Frontline Study (October 2025)
*Source: UKG, 8,200 frontline workers across 10 countries, survey conducted May–July 2025. businesswire.com*

- **76% of frontline workers** report burnout (consistent with 2024 UKG study)
- But: Workers who **use AI on the job report 41% burnout** vs. **54% for non-AI users** — a 13-point difference
- More than **1 in 3 frontline workers** (36%) now use AI in their roles
- AI anxiety remains high:
  - **2 in 3 worry** AI might replace their job
  - **1 in 4** say part of their job has already been replaced by AI
  - **1 in 5** believe their job will be completely replaced within five years
  - **65% fear** colleagues skilled in AI could take their job (the social threat, not just the machine)
  - **1 in 4** have lost trust in their employer over AI replacement concerns
- **85% say replacing frontline workers with AI would be a "huge mistake"**
- Nearly half are taking steps to "prove their worth" (learning new skills, extra projects, working sick)
- **1 in 3 say they would quit** if forced to use AI in ways that don't make sense

#### BCG "AI at Work 2025" (June 2025) — employee anxiety dimension
- Workers at organizations undergoing **comprehensive AI-driven workflow redesign are more worried** about job security (**46%**) vs. those at less-advanced AI companies (**34%**)
- **Leaders and managers (43%)** are more worried about losing their job in the next 10 years than frontline employees (36%) — *counter-intuitive finding*

#### Pew Research "U.S. Workers Are More Worried Than Hopeful" (February 2025)
*Source: Pew Research Center, survey of 5,273 employed U.S. adults, conducted Oct 7–13, 2024. pewresearch.org*

- **52% of workers are worried** about the future impact of AI in the workplace
- **33% feel overwhelmed**; only **36% feel hopeful**
- **32% think AI will lead to fewer job opportunities** for them in the long run
- Only **6% say AI will lead to more job opportunities** for them
- **16% of workers** currently have at least some work done with AI; **63% use AI little or not at all**
- Workers more likely to find AI helpful for **speed** (40% say extremely/very helpful) than for **quality** (29%)
- Lower- and middle-income workers are more likely than upper-income workers to expect fewer job opportunities

## Contradictions and Counterarguments

**These findings complicate or push back on the thesis that human skills are unambiguously ascending:**

### 1. Anxiety about human skills may be *rational*, not irrational
- **UKG finding:** 1 in 4 workers say part of their job has *already* been replaced by AI. If this is accurate at scale, the threat is real, not just perceived.
- **BCG finding:** Workers in organizations doing *more* advanced AI work are *more* worried about job security (46% vs. 34%) — suggesting deeper AI integration does not reliably reassure workers.

### 2. Most workers aren't using AI yet, creating a selection bias problem
- **PwC:** Only 14% use GenAI daily. Most of the optimistic data comes from daily users who are a self-selected, likely high-skill, high-adaptability group.
- **Pew:** 63% use AI little or not at all. We may be extrapolating from an enthusiast minority to the broader workforce.

### 3. The skills shift may be slower than reports suggest
- **HBR:** Skills half-life is shrinking, but the jobs requiring purely human skills haven't dramatically increased yet — the WEF and others are forecasting 2025–2030 trends, not describing a fully realized present.
- **Pew:** Only 6% of U.S. workers believe AI will *increase* their job opportunities. This gap between expert optimism and worker perception is real and significant.

### 4. Organizations are not actually hiring for human skills yet
- **SHRM:** 55% of employers find it the *most difficult* part of hiring to assess soft skills — and most still rely on resumes (67%) and unstructured interviews (77%). The gap between stated priority and actual practice is large.
- **TestGorilla 2025:** Skills-based hiring is growing but most organizations haven't operationalized it.

### 5. The manager engagement collapse complicates the "human leadership" narrative
- **Gallup 2025:** Manager engagement fell sharply, with female and young managers experiencing the worst declines. If the human skills advantage depends on emotionally intelligent managers, and those managers are burning out, the system has a structural fragility.

### 6. AI-assisted speed may matter more to workers than human quality
- **Pew:** Workers find AI more helpful for *speed* (40% extremely/very helpful) than *quality* (29%). If organizations optimize for throughput, human quality skills may be de-prioritized in practice even if valued in principle.

## Gaps in Available Data

1. **Direct causal evidence linking empathy/human skills → better AI outcomes is thin.** Most evidence is correlational or attitudinal. We do not yet have strong longitudinal data showing that companies with higher empathy culture outperform on AI adoption metrics. BCG's leadership support finding (15% → 55% positivity) is the strongest signal, but it measures sentiment, not business outcomes.

2. **Psychological safety as an AI predictor is under-quantified.** The Psychology Today and MIT Tech Review articles make the case persuasively, but the academic evidence base is still building. Edmondson's foundational work is solid, but specific to AI adoption it remains nascent.

3. **Deloitte Human Capital Trends 2025/2026** — Deloitte publishes an annual Human Capital Trends report that would be directly relevant. We found a January 2026 piece on high-performing teams but could not access the full data. ⚠️ Recommend verifying the 2025 and 2026 Deloitte Human Capital Trends reports directly.

4. **Industry-specific data is sparse.** Most findings are cross-industry. We don't know if human skills premiums differ between, say, healthcare (already human-intensive) vs. financial services (more automatable). PwC provides some industry cuts but the trend direction is consistent, not differentiated.

5. **The "kindness" or "care culture" → organizational performance link is not directly evidenced in these sources.** The Workday and WEF data support "empathy as a valued skill," not "organizations with kind cultures outperform on AI transformation." That specific claim requires a more targeted search or original research.

6. **Turnover data in AI-heavy organizations** is notably absent. We found burnout data (UKG) and disengagement data (Gallup) but not direct turnover/attrition statistics linked specifically to AI adoption quality.

## Recommendations: What Can Be Confidently Claimed

### ✅ High Confidence (well-evidenced, multiple sources)

1. **Human skills are becoming *more* valued, not less, as AI adoption grows.** WEF, Workday, SHRM, HBR, PwC all confirm this from different angles.

2. **The most in-demand human skills in 2025–2026 are:** analytical thinking, resilience/adaptability, creative thinking, leadership, empathy/relationship building, and ethical judgment.

3. **Worker anxiety about AI displacement is high, widespread, and not fully addressed by employers.** Pew (52% worried), UKG (2/3 worried about job loss), Gallup (engagement falling).

4. **Leadership support dramatically amplifies AI adoption.** BCG: 15% → 55% positive sentiment with strong leader support. This is the single most leverage-able human factor in AI adoption.

5. **Organizations that use AI well have better employee outcomes.** UKG: AI users have 41% burnout rate vs. 54% for non-users. PwC daily users report far higher productivity, quality, and job security confidence.

6. **Most organizations haven't cracked the code.** BCG: 51% frontline adoption ceiling. PwC: Only 14% daily usage. McKinsey: 46% cite skill gaps as the #1 barrier.

### ⚠️ Moderate Confidence (directionally consistent, less quantified)

7. **Psychological safety is a meaningful predictor of AI adoption quality.** Multiple sources point this direction, but direct causal quantification is limited to date.

8. **The gap between what leaders say they value and how they actually hire/develop people is large.** SHRM: 55% can't assess soft skills, yet 67% still rely on resumes.

9. **Manager relationships are the single biggest lever on team engagement — and managers are struggling.** Gallup data is robust; the AI-causation angle is inferential.

### ❌ Not Yet Supported (interesting thesis, data is thin)

10. **"Kind" or empathetic organizational cultures measurably outperform on AI transformation.** This framing is intellectually compelling but would require more specific research. Current data supports "human-centered" and "trust-based" cultures without specifically naming kindness/empathy as the operative variable at the organizational level.
