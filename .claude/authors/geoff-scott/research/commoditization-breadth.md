# AI Commoditization Breadth: Which Industries Are and Are NOT Racing to the Mean
## Research Report: 2024–2026 Data

*Compiled: March 2026 | Research focus: Actual current data, not predictions*

---

## Executive Summary

The commoditization wave is broader and faster than most analyses acknowledge. It is hitting virtually every knowledge/information/expertise-based business — not just tech. But the picture is more nuanced than "AI destroys everything." There's a clear bifurcation emerging between:

1. **Execution work** — being commoditized aggressively and rapidly
2. **Judgment, trust, relationships, and physical presence** — demonstrably resistant

The critical insight: AI isn't flattening all knowledge businesses equally. It's compressing the *execution layer* of every knowledge business while potentially increasing the premium on the *judgment/trust/leadership layer*. This creates a structural divide within industries, not just between them.

---

## Part 1: The Scale of the Knowledge Economy Being Affected

### How much of the economy is at risk?

- **U.S. knowledge workers generate $10–11 trillion in annual compensation** — roughly 40–45% of U.S. GDP (~$25 trillion) [Daniel Miessler/GitHub research]
- **Globally**: Knowledge worker compensation is approximately $70 trillion of a $110 trillion global GDP
- **76.18% of the U.S. workforce** holds knowledge-intensive employment (WIPO Global Innovation Index, 2024)
- A **2024 Brookings Institution study** found that based on existing technology, **30% of all U.S. workers could see at least half of their tasks automated** [AEI citation]
- **Goldman Sachs** estimates **40%+ of legal tasks** could be automated — which they called "a problem for firms that charge richly for juniors' time" (Reuters, July 2025)
- ONS UK found **27% of management consultant tasks face high automation probability**; McKinsey Global Institute estimates the figure could be as high as 45%

**The bottom line on scope**: We're talking about the commoditization pressure hitting 40–50% of U.S. GDP. This is not a sector story. It's a macroeconomic story.

## Part 2: Industries Under Active AI Commoditization Pressure (2024–2026)

### 2.1 Management Consulting — ACTIVELY DISRUPTING

**What's actually happening:**
- **McKinsey** announced in December 2025 plans to cut ~10% of its workforce across non-client-facing roles over the next 2 years. For context, headcount grew from ~17,000 to ~45,000 between 2012 and 2022 — this is the first significant reversal
- **Accenture** cut 22,000 jobs in 2025 while simultaneously expanding AI capabilities; Q1 FY2026 showed $2.2 billion in AI bookings alone
- Consulting industry departures increased nearly 7% in 2024
- Revenue growth slowed significantly: Deloitte's revenue growth rate dropped sharply from 14.9% to 3.1% before recovering slightly in 2025
- **89% of consulting buyers now expect AI incorporation** in delivered services
- **66% will stop working with providers that don't use AI** in their methodologies (IBM Institute for Business Value, 2024)

**What's being commoditized:**
Core junior consulting activities — research synthesis, financial modeling, competitive intelligence, PowerPoint deck creation, compliance checklists. "A large language model can now produce a decent competitive analysis, SWOT framework, or market entry strategy in minutes. What took a team of three junior consultants two weeks now takes ChatGPT four minutes." (ComplianceGenie analysis, Jan 2026)

**What's NOT being commoditized:**
AI implementation consulting is *booming*. The global AI consulting services market is projected to grow from **$11 billion in 2025 to $91 billion by 2035** (Future Market Insights). Boutique firms with deep domain expertise are thriving. Winning firms combine: technical AI acumen, domain expertise, implementation accountability.

**Contradiction to flag**: The industry is bifurcating, not collapsing. Big firms that built execution capabilities (Accenture, Deloitte, EY) are restructuring and surviving. Pure-strategy McKinsey-style consulting is under existential pressure.

### 2.2 Legal / Law Firms — COMPLEX (Higher End Growing, Lower End Commoditizing)

**What's actually happening:**
- **Am Law 100 posted 13.3% year-over-year revenue growth in 2024**, generating $158.3 billion in total revenue — suggesting the top end of the market is not suffering
- **Average law firm revenues rose 11.3% in the first half of 2025**, with partner profits up 13.7%
- **Billing rates jumped 9.2%**, with top partners now at record rates

**But underneath the headline numbers:**
- Goldman Sachs: **40%+ of legal tasks could be automated**
- Reuters (July 2025) titled: "AI dooms the billable hour – and Big Law earnings"
- Junior-associate-heavy work models are under structural pressure even if current revenues look fine
- AI legal services market growing from $1.7 billion in 2025 to $10.4 billion by 2035 — this is new entrants capturing work that was previously billed at $300-500/hr for junior time
- One startup model ("Pierson Ferdinand") explicitly built as a "junior-free" firm
- Thomson Reuters: Share of legal organizations actively integrating GenAI rose from 14% in 2024 to 26% in 2025 (doubling in one year)

**The contradiction**: Law firm revenues are currently UP because the productivity gains are being captured by existing partners (not passed to clients). The structural fragility is building — clients will eventually demand fee reductions the same way KPMG demanded them from Grant Thornton. The top of the market may hold (because high-stakes counsel requires trust/judgment), but volume legal work is being commoditized.

### 2.3 Accounting / Audit — ACTIVELY DISRUPTING

**What's actually happening:**
- **KPMG forced a 14% fee reduction** from its auditor Grant Thornton in 2025, explicitly citing AI-driven cost savings. The audit fee dropped from $416,000 (2024) to $357,000 (2025) — saving $59,000. The precedent this sets for all audit clients is significant
- **Big Four AI adoption in accounting jumped from 9% to 41%** in a single year (Wolters Kluwer Future Ready Accountant report, 2025)
- Accountancy graduates are being replaced by AI in UK; Big Four are scaling back graduate recruitment
- Big Four firms are cutting jobs while scaling AI; tech spending is now outpacing people spending
- Goldman Sachs partnered with Anthropic on a 6-month project to automate mundane accounting tasks

**The KPMG paradox**: By demanding AI savings from its own auditor, KPMG inadvertently showed all of its *own* clients how to demand the same from KPMG. The commoditization is now self-reinforcing.

**What's resistant**: High-stakes audit work requiring professional judgment and liability-bearing sign-off. Senior partners with deep client trust and regulatory expertise. But the pyramid model supporting this (lots of junior staff at lower rates) is under structural threat.

---

### 2.4 Media / Journalism / Content — SEVERE DISRUPTION UNDERWAY

**What's actually happening:**
- **~15,000 media jobs eliminated in 2024** (Challenger, Gray & Christmas)
- **17,000+ entertainment, news, streaming, and broadcast jobs cut in 2025** — an **18% increase** from the prior year (TheWrap analysis)
- **3,000+ journalism job cuts** tracked in UK and US in 2025
- **Writing projects on Upwork declined 32% year over year in 2025** — the largest drop of any category on the platform (Mediabistro, 2026)
- Within 8 months of ChatGPT's launch, demand for freelance writing jobs fell roughly **30%** — steepest decline of any category studied
- Job posting analysis (180M global jobs, 2023–2025): Writers -28%, photographers -28%, computer graphic artists -33% — and these are two-year declines, not one-year blips
- PR specialist jobs declined 21%; journalists/reporters declined 22%

**What IS growing:**
- **Influencer marketing specialists: +18.3% in 2025** (on top of +10% in 2024 — a two-year sustained trend)
- Creative directors and creative managers are relatively stable
- The insight: "Execution creative" is being commoditized; "trust-based human creator relationships" are growing

**The trust thesis (supported by data)**: Influencer marketing is growing because "as people flood the internet with AI content, traditional channels are losing what little trust they had left... But a skincare video from a TikTok creator their age? That still feels real and genuine." (Bloomberry analysis citing Rand Fishkin and Mark Schaefer, 2025)

### 2.5 SaaS / Software Companies — MAJOR BIFURCATION

**What's actually happening (stock market as leading indicator):**

*Winners in 2025:*
- Palantir: +142% (AI-native, mission-critical)
- Cloudflare: +80% (infrastructure)
- Vertical SaaS (Veeva: +36%, Guidewire: +27%)
- Cybersecurity (CrowdStrike: +51%, Zscaler: +34%, Palo Alto: +30%)

*Losers in 2025:*
- HubSpot: -51% (horizontal SMB SaaS)
- Adobe: -35% (creative tools)
- Salesforce: -31% (enterprise CRM)
- Monday.com: -36%
- Atlassian: -34%
- Bill.com: -43%

**The pattern**: Companies in "horizontal" categories where AI can replicate features are being punished. The specific risks:
1. Seat-based pricing under pressure (AI enables "same output with fewer seats")
2. SMB customers most vulnerable to AI disruption of their own businesses
3. Feature parity achievable in days/weeks by AI-native competitors

**AI-native startups are growing at 100%+ median** while traditional SaaS grows at 23% (Emergence Capital data cited by Deep Star Strategic)

**The vertical SaaS exception**: Companies deeply embedded in regulated industries with vertical integration (Veeva in life sciences, Guidewire in insurance) are performing well. Their moat is not features — it's deep workflow integration and regulatory knowledge.

### 2.6 Education — EARLY STAGE DISRUPTION, COMPLEX DYNAMICS

**What's actually happening:**
- AI tutoring market: $3.55 billion in 2025, forecast $6.45 billion by 2030
- AI in education market grew from $5.88 billion (2024) to $7.05 billion (2025), projected $136.79 billion by 2035 (Grand View Research)
- EdTech revenue multiples contracted from **17.6x in 2024 to 8.1x in 2025** — a massive compression in the *valuation* of traditional EdTech
- Higher education is grappling with AI-generated student work destroying traditional assessment models

**What's holding:**
- Physical campus experience (social formation, credential signaling, athletics/culture)
- Vocational/practical education (trades, clinical programs)
- High-touch tutoring and mentorship at premium price points

**The contradiction**: AI is flooding the low end of education (content delivery, basic tutoring) while potentially increasing demand for high-touch human mentorship at the premium end.

### 2.7 Financial Advisory / Wealth Management — MODERATE DISRUPTION

**What's actually happening:**
- AI-driven investment tools projected to become the **primary source of advice for retail investors by 2027** (World Economic Forum, March 2025), with 80% usage projected by 2028
- Wealth manager stocks tanked after Altruist launched new AI tool in 2025
- Robo-advisory growing; basic portfolio management being commoditized

**What's resistant:**
- Complex, holistic financial planning involving behavioral coaching, estate planning, tax integration
- Ultra-high-net-worth clients who want human accountability and relationship
- Behavioral finance — helping clients not panic sell is fundamentally a trust/relationship problem, not an information problem

**The professional designation moat**: Fiduciary liability and licensure requirements slow AI substitution even where capability exists. Regulators have not authorized AI to independently provide regulated financial advice.

### 2.8 Healthcare / Medicine — PARTIAL DISRUPTION, HEAVILY REGULATED

**What's actually happening:**
- AI in healthcare diagnostics market: $33.24 billion in 2025, projected $155.31 billion by 2029 (47% CAGR)
- AI in medical imaging: $2.57 billion in 2026, projected to grow significantly
- **Medical scribe job postings down 20%** — clearest current signal of actual job displacement
- 22% of healthcare organizations have implemented domain-specific AI tools (a 7x increase over 2024) [Menlo Ventures]

**What's NOT being disrupted (structurally protected):**
- Clinical licensure requirements — AI cannot independently prescribe, diagnose, or treat legally
- 47 states introduced 250+ AI bills in 2025; 33 passed; California explicitly **prohibits mental health providers from using AI to provide mental/behavioral health care**
- Physical examination, surgery, tactile diagnosis
- Doctor-patient relationship and informed consent

**The therapy/counseling wall**: This is a sector where regulation is actively blocking AI substitution. California SB 903 was introduced explicitly to keep "human-centered care" at the forefront of therapy and psychotherapy. Even where AI *could* approximate therapy, law is requiring humans.

---

### 2.9 Insurance — EARLY DISRUPTION, GROWING

**What's actually happening:**
- AI coming for insurance underwriting, claims processing, and actuarial work
- Insurance industry sits in a high-AI-exposure, low-adaptability-of-workers category (Insurance Business Magazine analysis)
- New AI tools for claims processing and underwriting growing rapidly
- Workers in insurance face both high exposure and low structural protection

**What's resistant:**
- Complex commercial insurance requiring judgment and relationship management
- Specialty lines requiring deep domain expertise
- State licensing requirements (like financial advisory)

## Part 3: Industries NOT Racing to the Mean (Structurally Resistant)

### 3.1 Skilled Trades — ACTIVELY GROWING, AI-PROOF

**The data:**
- **Demand for robotics technicians: +107%** since late 2022 (Randstand Report, March 2026)
- **HVAC engineers: +67%** since late 2022
- **Construction roles: +30%** since late 2022
- Electricians projected to grow **9% from 2024 to 2034** — more than double the average for all occupations (BLS)
- AI boom is actually *creating* more demand for skilled trades: data centers need electricians and HVAC technicians

**Why they're resistant:**
1. Physical presence absolutely required — cannot be delivered remotely
2. Tactile/spatial problem-solving AI cannot yet perform reliably
3. Licensing requirements in most jurisdictions
4. Capital-intensive equipment, infrastructure, and safety requirements
5. Massive current shortage — not a saturated labor market

**The AI creates demand dynamic**: The AI infrastructure buildout (data centers, power infrastructure, robotics systems) is *increasing* demand for skilled trades. This is a category where AI actively grows the market for human work rather than replacing it.

---

### 3.2 Cybersecurity — AI INCREASING DEMAND FOR HUMAN EXPERTISE

**The data:**
- Global cybersecurity market: ~$227.6 billion in 2025 (growing, not shrinking)
- AI in cybersecurity market valued at $5.35 billion in 2024 — projected significant growth
- Cybersecurity companies among the strongest performers in 2025: CrowdStrike +51%, Zscaler +34%, Palo Alto +30%

**Why it's growing despite AI:**
AI is creating a more sophisticated threat landscape, not a less complex one. As AI enables more sophisticated attacks, it simultaneously increases demand for human expertise to defend against them. More attack surface + more AI-generated attacks = more demand for security expertise. This is a rare category where AI is *increasing* market size rather than commoditizing services.

---

### 3.3 Senior Leadership / Strategic Decision-Making — RESILIENT

**The data:**
- Senior leadership roles declined only -1.7% in 2025 vs. -8% for the overall job market
- Individual contributor roles declined -9%
- Directors and above are among the fastest-growing job categories:
  - Director, Data Engineering: +23%
  - Director, Legal: +21%
  - Director, Software Engineering: +14%
  - VP, Engineering: +12%

**Why it's resistant:**
AI is actually *empowering* senior leaders to operate more independently. A VP who can now prototype with AI coding tools, analyze markets in minutes, and draft strategies rapidly doesn't need as many ICs. The "what to do" layer is not commoditized — the "how to execute it" layer is.

---

### 3.4 Mental Health / Therapy — LEGALLY PROTECTED

**Active regulatory protection:**
- California SB 903 explicitly prohibits AI from providing mental/behavioral health care
- 47 states introduced 250+ AI healthcare bills in 2025; many specifically protecting clinical human relationships
- The "embodied therapist" thesis: AI can simulate empathy, but "what it can't do is know suffering from the inside" (clinical psychology perspective)
- Kaiser's plans to use AI for therapy have generated significant backlash and legal/regulatory scrutiny

**The nuance**: AI *tools* for therapists are growing (documentation, session prep, psychoeducation), but AI *substituting* for the therapist-patient relationship is being actively blocked by regulation and professional standards. The therapy relationship itself appears culturally and legally protected — at least in the near term.

---

### 3.5 High-Touch Luxury / Hospitality — HUMANS AS PREMIUM

**The thesis (emerging empirical evidence):**
- "Humans-as-Luxury": As automation becomes the default, human presence becomes a premium feature
- Luxury hospitality is reframing itself around the scarcity of authentic human service
- Analogy to artisanal goods: As mass production floods the market with identical products, handmade commands a premium

**The three hospitality models emerging** (Hospitality Net, April 2025):
1. **Technocentric** — budget hotels, fully automated, compete on price
2. **Hybrid** — AI handles transactions, humans handle meaningful interactions
3. **Anthropocentric** — staffed entirely by humans as a luxury differentiator

**What's happening**: Hospitality wages rose 29% from mid-2019 to mid-2023 — overtaking the 20% average increase in higher-paying sectors. Scarcity of authentic human service is driving wage premiums.

---

### 3.6 Creator Economy / Authentic Human Content — GROWING (Paradoxically)

**The data:**
- Influencer marketing specialist jobs grew **+18.3% in 2025** (on top of +10% in 2024) — a sustained two-year trend
- This is happening simultaneously with massive AI content flooding all traditional channels

**The mechanism (supported by analysis from Rand Fishkin, Sparktoro):**
"Trust in businesses and advertising continues to erode, but we trust each other. Influencers are not just young people shilling products. They are viewed as trusted online friends, and that is a powerful voice for brands to connect to and leverage."

The AI content flood is paradoxically increasing the *relative value* of authentic human content and relationships. As AI-generated content becomes ubiquitous, human authenticity becomes scarce and valuable.

---

### 3.7 Physical/Embodied Services (Healthcare, Childcare, Elder Care)

**Why resistant:**
- Physical examination requires presence and touch
- Child development and elder care require embodied relationship and caregiving
- Therapeutic presence is legally and ethically defined as requiring a human
- Physical therapy, occupational therapy, massage — require dexterous physical interaction

**Data point**: While medical scribes are declining (-20%), medical assistants and clinical roles are holding steady or growing slightly.

## Part 4: The Structural Taxonomy of AI Resistance

Based on the research, here are the categories of businesses that are structurally resistant to AI commoditization:

### Category 1: Physical Presence Required
- Skilled trades (electrical, plumbing, HVAC)
- Physical healthcare (surgery, physical therapy)
- Childcare / elder care
- Construction and infrastructure
- *Why resistant*: Cannot be delivered digitally. Period.

### Category 2: Trust/Relationship Is the Product
- High-touch therapy and counseling
- Pastoral care, spiritual direction
- High-stakes personal financial advising
- Personal coaching at premium levels
- *Why resistant*: The human relationship is not a delivery mechanism — it IS the service. You can't "AI" the therapeutic alliance.

### Category 3: Regulated with Human Requirements
- Licensed clinical mental health care (California, others actively legislating)
- Medical diagnosis and prescription
- Legal representation in court
- Fiduciary financial advice
- *Why resistant*: Regulation explicitly requires human judgment and accountability. AI may augment but cannot substitute legally.

### Category 4: AI Increases Market Size (Threat Landscape Expansion)
- Cybersecurity
- AI governance/compliance consulting
- *Why resistant*: AI creates the problem, which increases demand for human expertise to solve it.

### Category 5: Scarcity Premium (Authenticity as Luxury)
- Human-staffed luxury hospitality
- Artisanal/craft goods
- Authentic creator content (influencers, speakers)
- *Why resistant*: As AI floods the low-value-human-touch segment, authentic human interaction becomes scarce and commands a premium.

### Category 6: Strategic Decision-Making + Accountability
- Senior leadership / C-suite
- Board governance
- High-stakes judgment calls with fiduciary responsibility
- *Why resistant*: AI can generate options and analysis but cannot hold legal/ethical accountability. Board members cannot be AI.

## Part 5: The Paradox Pattern — What Looks Commoditized But Isn't (Yet)

### Law (High End)
Top law firms are *growing* revenues and *raising* billing rates. This is because they're capturing the productivity gains from AI for partner profits. But the structural pressure is building: eventually clients will demand fee compression the way KPMG demanded it from Grant Thornton. The question is when, not if.

### Medicine
AI diagnostics are arriving, but physician reimbursement hasn't compressed yet. Regulatory and licensure barriers are holding. However, the radiologist and pathologist roles are under more immediate pressure than primary care (the physical exam + relationship moat doesn't exist for image reading).

### Software Engineering
Massive hype about AI replacing developers, but job postings are actually relatively stable (-roughly in line with overall market). AI tools appear to be making developers more productive, not redundant — at least for complex development. Simple frontend work is declining.

## Part 6: Key Contradictions and Tensions

1. **Top of market vs. bottom of market**: Premium-positioned services in nearly every industry are showing resilience while volume/commodity services are collapsing. The bifurcation is not just between industries — it's within them.

2. **The productivity capture question**: In law and accounting, the productivity gains from AI are currently being captured by senior partners/shareholders, not passed to clients. This is not sustainable indefinitely — clients will eventually demand fee compression (the KPMG/Grant Thornton precedent is a leading indicator).

3. **Execution vs. judgment**: AI is replacing execution work across virtually every knowledge industry simultaneously. It's not (yet) replacing judgment, contextual wisdom, accountability, or relationship. But the line between "execution" and "judgment" will keep moving.

4. **The trust paradox**: AI flooding of content and services is *increasing* the relative value of human trust and authenticity in select categories (influencer marketing, luxury hospitality, therapy). The commoditization wave creates its own counter-wave of premium human-touch demand.

5. **Regulation as temporary moat**: Mental health, medical, financial advisory, and legal fields are partially protected by regulation. But regulation is a delay mechanism, not a permanent barrier — it can change, and AI capabilities will continue advancing.

6. **The AI consulting boom within consulting's collapse**: Traditional strategy consulting is under existential pressure, but AI consulting is projected to grow from $11B to $91B by 2035. The same disruption that's destroying old consulting is creating new consulting demand.
